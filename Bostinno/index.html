7 Alternatives to Google Reader

Walter Frick June 24th 2013, 10:48am
Tech , Google
+1

It's getting perilously close to July 1, the day Google will turn off access to Reader, its RSS reading product. The initial announcement of the product's sunsetting set off a fervor, particularly in media and tech circles where lots of RSS loyalists remain. If you're a Reader user—or even if you're not—it's worth considering the alternatives out there for you when Google Reader goes away.

Earlier lists of alternatives were a good start, but since the Google announcement, a whole new class of readers have started to be developed, some of which aren't out yet. So it's worth revisiting the field.

Twitter

The first thing everyone asks me about the end of Reader is whether I'll bother to replace it at all. Why not just use Twitter? There's something to that, but for now, Twitter remains far too noisy to function as a substitute. If you do want to go all Twitter, you're going to have to use Lists heavily, and I recommend checking out the Discover tab. My guess is that Twitter will take the filtering of Discover, combine it with lists, and some time in the future have a powerful RSS alternative. But for now, it's not good enough.

The closest alternative: Feedly

Based on my informal Twitter polling, the most popular current Google Reader alternative is, by far, Feedly. It boasts a clean interface, lets you import feeds from Reader, and—importantly—it's ready to go today. Right now, it may be the best bet.

The visual choice: Flipboard

If you're willing to try something a bit different, check out Flipboard. It won't look like Google Reader, but rather approximates the digital version of a glossy magazine with its focus on images. It, too, will let you import your feeds.

The local choice: Curata

If you're looking to support a Boston startup, check out Curata's new reader. I covered it a while back, and have since been told by founder Pawan Deshpande that the company has made some improvements to handle the volume of interest they received. Curata's main product is a tool to let businesses aggregate relevant content for their industry.

In development...

Digg

Some of the most promising Reader alternatives are still in development. Notably, the company that now owns the news aggregator Digg, Betaworks, has promised to have its RSS reader out by July 1. Given the team's success with the Digg redesign, this is a hotly anticipated option. Keep tabs on its release here, and learn more about the process here.

Aol

Miss getting the internet through a filter tightly controlled by Aol? Didn't think so. Nonetheless, the company has thrown its hat in the ring and will soon have a reader on the market. Check out this review for more details. It looks cool, I must say, though I'm not sure I can bring myself to put my feeds in Aol's hands. Sign up here.

Facebook

Facebook is basically the last place I'd like to do all my reading, but perhaps you feel differently. The company is reportedly hard at work on a reader alternative, and has been for a while. It will be Flipboard-esque and mobile-focused. While I doubt it'll be a favorite among the power user crowd, with Facebook's product talent it'll probably be fast and pretty, and may have the biggest chance of gaining widespread adoption of anything here.

Other alternatives out there that I missed? Let me know. Will you be sticking with RSS? Going all Twitter? Something else? I look forward to hearing in the comments.
