A picture is worth a thousand words – especially during an Investor Hangout

August 15, 2013





Learning to deliver a pitch in only 300 seconds is a valuable skill for entrepreneurs.

By Paolo Gaudiano (Founder, Infomous)

On July 12 I had the pleasure of participating in a Women 2.0 Investor Hangout, where I presented Infomous to Kathleen Utecht of Comcast Ventures and Morgane Rollando of Synerfia. I was excited for this opportunity because I know how valuable it is to be forced to give a “lightning” pitch: investors are flooded with requests from enthusiastic entrepreneurs, but they need to be able to understand your idea in a short time. How do you condense all the excitement, the opportunity, the value, the evidence into only five minutes?

In an interesting parallel, Infomous was created to make it easy to explore large amounts of content. Specifically, Infomous can collect articles, blogs, tweets and other types of text, and makes it easy for users to find quickly what is relevant to them. For example, the Infomous cloud above this article shows the main topics appearing across dozens of articles from the women2.com. As you click different words you will reveal links to related articles.

The success of our platform is based on the notion that humans prefer visual exploration to reading – as the old adage goes, a picture is worth a thousand words. My task was to apply the same notion when presenting Infomous during the Investor Hangout.

Before the Hangout took place, I thought carefully about what I wanted to achieve: make a convincing case that we are tackling a huge problem; show that we have developed an elegant solution; demonstrate tangible progress; illustrate our potential as a business. How could I pack so much content in only 300 seconds?

Many years ago, I was invited to present my PhD research at a prestigious conference. I had prepared 15 slides and had rehearsed until I could go through my presentation in the allotted five minutes. How did I condense years of research down to five minutes? I did it by speaking as fast as the folks who read legal disclaimers at the end of radio ads. After my breathtaking presentation, a leading researcher in that field got up and congratulated me for breaking all records for the number of words uttered in five minutes. There were no other questions from the audience. During a follow-up conversation, the same gentleman advised me that my presentation was so overwhelming as to be virtually incomprehensible.

That day I learned an important lesson: if you have a good idea you should be able to state it convincingly in just a few words. If you feel compelled to say a ton of things, chances are that you are not entirely clear about what is truly valuable and compelling about your own idea. This is why I would encourage everyone to try a lightning pitch, and why I am so grateful to Women 2.0 for creating this opportunity for many entrepreneurs.

My experience with the Investor Hangout pitch was great: the two investors gave me valuable feedback and advice. For instance, they pointed out that I should have had a slide with overall market size, and I should have talked about the team up front. These were silly mistakes on my part because all investors care about the team and want a sense of the financial potential. I should have placed the team slide up front, and could have shown a visual depiction of the market size in less than 15 of my precious 300 seconds.

In closing, I offer the following advice for those doing Investor Hangouts or similar short, online pitches:

  • Minimize the number of words on each slide, relying more on pictures, icons, or other visual items.
  • Have a detailed plan for your pitch, but be prepared to be flexible as you may not get through all your slides, or someone may ask you a question.
  • Expect technical problems – but don’t expect that you will get extra time to solve them. Be prepared to deliver your pitch even without slides or a demo.
  • For online pitches keep in mind that there is a delay between what you see and what your audience is seeing. Avoid animations and pause frequently to make sure the audience is in sync.
  • Finally, when your time is up, stop! Going over time shows that you were not prepared adequately and may annoy your audience. You should be able to stop anywhere and make it seem like that was exactly where you wanted to end.
